Education Choices with Melbourne Secondary School Metrics (2022)


Figure 1: Distribution of Secondary Schools by Gender

In 2022, roughly 85% of all secondary schools are coeducational (schools of mixed genders) while it is 11% for girls school and only 4% for boys school. There exists diversity of choices but the choices of all boys school are lower than all girls.

In Figure 1,those percentages are shown in human figure where each coloured figure represents 1% of all secondary schools in a grid of 100 figures - giving 100 percent. Blue figure shows boys schools; pink figure shows girls school while taupe figure shows mixed gender schools.

Figure 2: Density of ICSEA Scores

It is advised to compare schools with similar socio-educational advantages for fair evaluation. Therefore, the Index of Community Socio-Educational Advantage (ICSEA) comes into picture as a scale typically ranging from 500 (extremely disadvantaged) to 1300 (utmost priviliged).

ICSEA is based on a calculation of factors in socio-educational advantage (SEA), school location remoteness, and percentage of Indigenous student enrolment. SEA alone includes parental occupation, school education, and non-school education.

Through Figure 2, catholic schools are likely to score at around 1060 while government & independent secondary schools tends to score 970. This translates to catholic schools having higher probability of better socio-educational advantage than schools from other sectors.


Figure 3: Correlation of ICSEA Percentile with Teaching Staff

It is IMPORTANT that ICSEA should not be used as school performance rating since calculation for ICSEA does not consider parents' income or school teaching resources. This can be emphasised through comparing ICSEA percentile (a ranking scale on Australian ICSEA) with number of teaching staff in Figure 3. The crisscross of lines between ICSEA percentile and Number of Teaching Staff

indicates mixed correlation as there exists schools (mainly under government) with low ICSEA percentile having more teaching staff than those with HIGHER ICSEA percentile. As with the rest, ICSEA scales uniformly to its percentile and so are the majority of cases between number of teaching staff and total enrolments of students (its trend will be elaborated more in Figure 4 below).

Figure 4: Teacher to Student ratio

As seen previously, majority of the relationship between total number of teaching staff and student enrolment are proportional scale in horizontal lines. As calculated, Melbourne secondary schools have roughly 10 students assigned to one teacher regardless of its sector, according to Figure 4. This can be visualised in concentration of dots, forming an upward linear trend of 1:10 .

The trend of 1:10 is demonstrated through scaling number of teachers against number of students in groups of 10 with each dot representing a school. The ratio begins to diverge away from the trend at 2000 students. Schools on all sectors have a fair share on teaching staff number depending on number of student enrolment and NOT its ICSEA score.

Figure 5: Indigenous Students Enrolment Percentage

With 0.5% of Melbourne population belonging to Indigenous community, it is crucial to have good community support and cultural connection for indigenous children as part of their growth and identity. Hence, identifying suburbs with relatively high indigenous enrolment would be a key start in the selection process. In Figure 5 of the Greater Melbourne area, percentage of indigenous student enrolment in secondary

schools per suburb is coloured coded on a gradient scale. Suburbs that are in light gray either have no secondary school or lack enrolment data. Indigenous enrolment increases the further you are from the Central Business District (CBD). The suburb closest to CBD with enrolment percentage above 8% would be Kensington (9%).
